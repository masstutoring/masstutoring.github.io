Start here: understanding the SAT

You don't need a plan, a tutor, or any prior knowledge to use this page. By the end, you'll know how the test works and exactly what to do first.

What the SAT is (in one minute)

The SAT is a standardized test used in college admissions, taken on a computer using College Board's Bluebook app. It has two sections:

  • Reading and Writing — short passages with one question each, testing reading comprehension, vocabulary in context, grammar, and revision skills.
  • Math — algebra, advanced math, problem-solving and data analysis, and geometry/trigonometry. A built-in Desmos graphing calculator is available for the entire Math section.

Each section has two timed modules. The test is adaptive: how you do on the first module of a section determines whether your second module is easier or harder. Harder second modules unlock higher score ranges — so a rough start caps your ceiling, which is why consistent accuracy on early questions matters.

Scores range from 400 to 1600 (200–800 per section). The whole test takes a bit over two hours, with a short break between sections.

Details change. Exact timing, question counts, and policies are maintained by College Board. For current specifics, use the official resources page — we link directly to the source rather than repeating numbers that can go stale. This overview was last reviewed July 15, 2026.

Should you take the SAT?

Many colleges are test-optional, some require scores, and policies differ school by school and year by year. A strong score can help your application and scholarship chances even at test-optional schools; a score below a college's typical range can simply be withheld at most of them. If you're unsure, our honest suggestion: take one free practice test, see where you stand, and read our guide to score-submission decisions. The SAT is not a measure of your intelligence or worth — it's one data point that you can meaningfully improve with practice.

Your first three steps

  1. Learn the test (you're doing it now)

    Finish this page. That's genuinely the whole first step — most students skip it and waste weeks studying the wrong things.

  2. Take one official diagnostic practice test

    Install Bluebook (opens in a new tab), and take one full-length practice test under real conditions: timed, no music, no phone, one sitting. Your score will probably be lower than you'd like. That's normal and useful — it's a baseline, not a verdict.

  3. Review the test and pick your weakest areas

    Go through every question you missed and every question you guessed on. Sort your mistakes: which topic? content gap, misread, or time pressure? Those categories become your study plan. Our practice-test review guide walks through this step by step.

Then build your plan

Once you have a baseline score and a list of weak areas:

  • Choose a target score. Look up the middle 50% score range of a few colleges you're interested in; aiming at or above the middle of that range is a reasonable target.
  • Choose a test date that gives you time to prepare — for most students, two to three months of consistent study. See registration and test dates.
  • Pick a study plan matched to your timeline from our study plans page.
  • Drill weaknesses using the Math and Reading & Writing pages, and retest every few weeks with another Bluebook test.
