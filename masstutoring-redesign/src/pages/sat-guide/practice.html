Practice questions and practice tests

Practice is where scores actually move — but only when it's the right practice, reviewed the right way. Official questions first, always.

The order of prominence

  1. College Board Question Bank — official questions, filterable by skill. Your targeted-practice home.
  2. Bluebook full-length tests — official, adaptive, realistic. Your measuring stick.
  3. Khan Academy — official practice partner; structured lessons plus drills.
  4. Verified third-party platforms — extra volume once you've used the official well.

Official vs. third-party matters. Third-party questions — however good — are imitations. They're useful for extra reps, but only official questions reliably match real difficulty and style, and only official adaptive tests give trustworthy score estimates. If a website's questions feel much harder or easier than Bluebook, trust Bluebook.

Where to practice

How often should you take a full practice test?

For most students: one full-length test every two to three weeks during active prep, plus one in the final week as a dress rehearsal. Between tests, spend your time on targeted drilling — that's where improvement comes from. Testing more often than weekly usually means you're measuring instead of improving.

Don't burn official tests. Bluebook has a limited set of full-length tests (10+ at our last review, July 2026). Each one you take without properly reviewing is gone. Take a test only when you have time to review it afterward — the review is worth more than the test.

How to review a practice test (the part everyone skips)

  1. Recreate real conditions when you test

    Timed, one sitting, no phone, no pausing, scratch paper only. A test taken casually gives you a number you can't trust.

  2. Review every miss — and every lucky guess

    For each one, answer two questions: What is the correct reasoning? and Why did I miss it? Read the official explanation; if it doesn't click, look up the topic in the Math or Reading & Writing guide.

  3. Categorize each mistake

    Content gap · misread the question · misread a choice · careless calculation · time pressure · Desmos-entry error · grammar-rule gap · vocabulary gap · strategy error · blind guess. The category determines the fix: content gaps need lessons; careless errors need process changes; timing problems need pacing drills.

  4. Turn categories into next week's plan

    Your two or three most common categories become your drill topics in the Question Bank (opens in a new tab) until the next test.

  5. Track it in an error log

    One row per miss: topic, mistake category, the rule or move you should have used. Reread the log before every test. See the error log template.

Older practice material

PDFs of retired paper SATs float around online. Some of that material can still be useful drilling for reading and grammar fundamentals, but it doesn't match the current digital format, timing, or adaptive structure — and question style has shifted. If you use older material at all, treat it as supplemental skill practice, never as a score predictor, and make sure it's from a legal source. When in doubt, there is more than enough current official material above.
