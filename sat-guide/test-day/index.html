Registration, test day, and logistics

The boring logistics are where avoidable disasters live. This page covers registration through score release — linking to official sources for anything that can change.

Policies change. Fees, deadlines, device rules, and ID requirements are set by College Board and can be updated at any time. We link to the official pages and note that this page was last reviewed July 15, 2026 — always confirm details at the source before your test.

Registering

  1. Pick a date from the official test-date calendar (opens in a new tab) that leaves you enough prep time (see study plans) and lands before your college deadlines.
  2. Register early at the official registration page (opens in a new tab) — popular centers fill up weeks before the deadline. Registering early is the single best way to avoid a long drive on test morning.
  3. Can't afford the fee? Check fee-waiver eligibility (opens in a new tab) with your school counselor — waivers cover registration and more.
  4. Need accommodations? Start the accommodations process (opens in a new tab) well before registering — approval takes time and usually goes through your school's SSD coordinator.

If your preferred center is full: check nearby dates, check again periodically (seats open when others cancel), and expand your search radius before settling for a far-away center.

Device preparation (do this the week before)

  • Install and update Bluebook (opens in a new tab) on the device you'll test with (your own laptop/tablet, or a school-managed device — check the official device requirements).
  • Sign in and complete the exam setup when prompted — it downloads your test and issues your admission ticket.
  • Charge fully the night before; bring a charger. Battery must last the full test.
  • If you can't bring a device, College Board has a device-lending process — request one well in advance through the registration site.

Test day

  • Bring: your testing device + charger, admission ticket, acceptable photo ID, approved calculator if you want a physical backup (Desmos is built in), snacks and water for the break.
  • Leave at home / in your bag: phones and smartwatches (powered off and away per staff instructions), notes, and anything that beeps.
  • Arrive early — doors typically close before the listed start time. Plan the drive beforehand.
  • If something goes wrong (device failure, disruption, illness), tell the proctor immediately — they have official procedures, and College Board's policies cover verified disruptions.

Scores

Scores typically arrive within a couple of weeks in your College Board account — see official score information (opens in a new tab) for release dates, reading your report, and sending scores to colleges (fee-waiver students get unlimited free score sends).

How many times should you take the SAT?

There's no universally correct number. A useful default pattern:

  • Diagnose with practice tests, not real ones. Your "first attempt" should be a free Bluebook test at home, not a paid Saturday morning.
  • Plan for one to three real attempts. Many students take it twice: once with solid prep, and once more after targeted improvement. Scores often rise on a second attempt.
  • Retake when something will change. A retake makes sense if you have a concrete plan (new weaknesses identified, more prep time) or your score clearly underperformed your practice average — say, from test-day nerves or logistics problems.
  • Expect diminishing returns. Fourth and fifth attempts without new preparation rarely move scores meaningfully, and each attempt costs a Saturday, a fee (unless waived), and stress-budget better spent on grades and the rest of your life.
  • Superscoring helps retakers. Many colleges combine your best section scores across test dates ("superscoring"), which lowers the risk of a retake — but policies vary, so check each college's admissions site.

Should you submit your score? (test-optional, explained)

  • Test-optional colleges consider scores if submitted but don't require them. Test-required colleges require them. Test-blind/test-free colleges won't look at them at all. Policies change year to year — always verify on each college's own admissions page for your application cycle.
  • A common rule of thumb: submit if your score is at or above the middle of the college's published middle-50% range; consider withholding if it's below the 25th percentile. It's a heuristic, not a law.
  • Scores can also matter for scholarships and specific programs even at test-optional schools — check before deciding not to test at all.
  • College Board's Score Choice lets you choose which test dates to send, and superscore policies vary by college. Confirm both on official pages before sending.

ACT or SAT?

Colleges that accept one accept the other — there's no admissions penalty for choosing either. The honest way to decide is empirical:

  1. Take one free official practice test of each (SAT via Bluebook; ACT via the ACT's official free practice materials).
  2. Compare percentile performance, not raw feelings — then pick the test where you naturally score higher and prep for just that one.

Broad differences: the ACT includes a science-reasoning component and generally moves faster per question; the SAT gives more time per question and has Desmos built in. Neither is universally easier. Whichever you pick, commit — splitting prep across both tests usually hurts more than it helps.
